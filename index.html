Meet Dr. Susan Wilson, MBE

"Tumaini" means Hope in Swahili — and hope is exactly what Dr. Wilson has brought to Kagera for over 20 years.

In 2001, Dr. Susan Wilson and her husband traveled to Kagera, in northwest Tanzania, to work as medical doctors. They were devastated by the abject poverty and the 200,000 children orphaned by the AIDS crisis — a crisis intensified when HIV rates surged from 2% to 30% after widespread violence by militias in the 1990s.

Dr. Wilson dedicated her life to these children, founding the Tumaini Fund UK in 2003 and the Tumaini Fund USA in 2009. Today, 25,000 orphans are being supported — and the goal is to reach every one of the 200,000.

In 2013, Dr. Wilson was awarded the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire (MBE) by Prince William for her outstanding service.

Obstacles Overcome

Hope Is Winning

Real challenges. Real solutions. Real voices from Kagera.

01

Education

Children had no path to school — no uniforms, no books, no pencils. The average family income is $95 per year. Now, 25,000 orphans are being educated through Tumaini's sponsorship programs.

"I had no hope to progress but now I have a career. I started being sponsored by Tumaini at the beginning of secondary school. I would not be a teacher now if it wasn't for the support I have been given. God bless you."
— Jackson Samweli Education
02

Clean Water

Villages relied on filthy ponds shared with animals. 24,000 children under five die each year in Tanzania from dirty water. Tumaini has built over 60 wells, bringing clean water to entire communities.

"The shallow well in Kanyinya has reduced the distance we have to carry water. The health of our children is much better and so our medical bills have reduced. The children have better attendance at school."
— Committee Members, Kanyinya Parish Water
03

Disease Prevention

Malaria kills nearly 3,000 African children every day. A single mosquito net — costing just $10 — can protect a child's life. Tumaini has distributed thousands of nets and built homes with mosquito-proof windows.

"I am so grateful for the mosquito nets, three of my children have Malaria and it costs a lot of money for their treatment. I have two other children and I hope the net will prevent them getting sick."
— Mariamu Health
04

Building Futures

Orphans with no family had no future beyond subsistence farming. Today, Tumaini-sponsored students are becoming doctors, teachers, nurses, and skilled tradespeople — leaders of their communities.

"My name is Elia Ethan and I have been supported by the Tumaini Fund through secondary school. I am now a first year medical student and I am very happy to be able to go to University in Dar Es Salaam. Without support I would have to stay in my village and work on the land. I want to become a Psychiatrist."
— Elia Ethan, Medical Student Education
